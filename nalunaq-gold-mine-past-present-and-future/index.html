Nalunaq Gold Mine: Past, Present, and Future

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

nalunaqgreenland goldmine restarthistory

Nalunaq is Greenland's only gold mine to date, and its history provides important lessons for any future gold mining development in the territory. Located in South Greenland near the town of Nanortalik, Nalunaq operated from 2004 to 2013 and produced approximately 350,000 ounces of gold. Its story is a mix of geological promise, operational challenge, and the harsh economics of remote Arctic mining.

Discovery and Early Development

Gold mineralization at Nalunaq was identified in the early 1990s through regional exploration programs. The deposit is a narrow, high-grade quartz vein system hosted in Proterozoic granite and gneiss. The vein system strikes approximately north-south and extends for over 1,000 meters along strike and to depths exceeding 400 meters.

Crew Gold Corporation acquired the project and advanced it through feasibility study and permitting in the late 1990s and early 2000s. The project was attractive due to its high gold grades (averaging 15-20 grams per tonne gold in the mined zones) and Greenland's stable political jurisdiction.

Construction began in 2003, and commercial production was achieved in 2004. The mine was developed as an underground operation using a decline access system, with ore processed through a gravity-flotation circuit.

Production History

Nalunaq's production history is characterized by high grades but operational challenges:

  • **Total production**: Approximately 350,000 ounces of gold over the mine's life
  • **Peak production**: Approximately 55,000 ounces per year at peak
  • **Average grade**: Approximately 15-20 g/t gold, making Nalunaq a high-grade operation
  • **Recovery**: Gold recovery through gravity and flotation averaged approximately 90-95%
  • **Mining method**: Longhole stoping and shrinkage stoping in narrow vein geometry

    • The high gold grade compensated for the mine's remote location and higher-than-average operating costs, which were estimated at approximately US$1,200-1,300 per ounce.

    Challenges That Led to Closure

    Nalunaq was suspended in 2013 due to a combination of factors:

  • **Declining gold prices**: Gold prices fell from above US$1,700/oz in 2012 to below US$1,300/oz in 2013, squeezing margins
  • **Narrow vein complexity**: The vein geometry was irregular, with pinch-and-swell structures and varying dip angles that made consistent mining difficult
  • **Grade variability**: Gold grades were more variable than expected, with periods of lower-than-forecast production
  • **Remote logistics**: South Greenland's limited infrastructure required expensive air and sea logistics for supplies and concentrate shipping
  • **Operational costs**: Labor, energy, and supply costs were higher than comparable operations in more accessible locations

    • The combination of falling gold prices and persistent operational challenges made the mine uneconomic under the ownership structure at that time.

    Post-Closure Activity

    Since the 2013 suspension, several developments have kept Nalunaq in play:

  • **Ownership changes**: The project passed through several owners, including Aappaluttoq Greenland, a company backed by Australian investors
  • **Restart studies**: Additional drilling and metallurgical testing were conducted to evaluate restart potential
  • **Infrastructure**: The existing decline, tailings facility, and surface infrastructure provide a head start for any future restart
  • **Environmental monitoring**: Ongoing environmental monitoring at the site maintains regulatory compliance

    • Restart Potential at Current Gold Prices

    At gold prices above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026), Nalunaq's economics are substantially improved compared to when it closed:

  • **Revenue increase**: At US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026), gold revenue per tonne of ore is approximately 50-60% higher than at the US$1,300/oz price when the mine closed
  • **Cost increase**: Operating costs have likely increased due to inflation, but the percentage increase is smaller than the revenue increase
  • **Grade targeting**: A restart could focus on the highest-grade portions of the vein system, further improving economics
  • **Existing infrastructure**: The decline, tailings facility, and surface infrastructure reduce restart capital requirements significantly

    • A Nalunaq restart could potentially be achieved with US$30-50 million in capital expenditure (compared to US$100+ million for a greenfield development), with operating costs of US$1,400-1,600 per ounce and gold revenue of US$2,000+ per ounce, providing attractive margins.

    Lessons for Skaergaard and Other Greenland Projects

    Nalunaq's history offers important lessons:

  • **Grade matters**: High-grade deposits can overcome Arctic cost premiums. Skaergaard's multi-metal grade profile is favorable
  • **Infrastructure is an asset**: Existing infrastructure reduces both capital requirements and development risk for restart operations
  • **Metal prices drive viability**: The difference between closure at US$1,300/oz and restart potential at US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026) illustrates how metal prices control Arctic mining economics
  • **Narrow veins are challenging**: Vein deposits like Nalunaq are inherently more difficult to mine than disseminated deposits like Skaergaard. Skaergaard's open-pit potential is a significant advantage
  • **Jurisdiction works**: Nalunaq demonstrated that mining can be permitted and operated under Greenland's regulatory framework

    • Conclusion

    Nalunaq remains a significant asset in Greenland's mining portfolio. At current gold prices, the restart economics are more favorable than at any point since the mine was operating. While the vein geometry and remote location remain challenges, the existing infrastructure and high-grade potential make Nalunaq a viable candidate for restart. For the broader Greenland mining sector, Nalunaq's experience demonstrates both the challenges and the potential of Arctic gold mining.
