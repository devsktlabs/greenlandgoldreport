The Case for Gold in a Critical Minerals Portfolio

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

goldcritical mineralsportfolioinvestment strategy

The concept of a "critical minerals portfolio" typically brings to mind lithium, cobalt, rare earths, and other metals deemed essential for energy transition and defense applications. Gold rarely makes the list. However, there is a strong case for including gold exposure in a critical minerals investment strategy, both as a strategic hedge and as a complementary return driver.

Gold's Strategic Importance

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Gold may not be classified as a "critical mineral" under most government frameworks (which typically focus on metals essential to technology, energy, or defense supply chains), but it holds strategic importance in several ways:

  • **Monetary reserve asset**: Gold is the second-largest reserve asset held by central banks globally, behind only the US dollar. Central bank gold reserves provide monetary sovereignty and reduce dependence on any single currency
  • **Currency backing**: In a scenario of major currency disruption, gold serves as a universally accepted medium of exchange and store of value
  • **Systemic hedge**: Gold has historically performed well during periods of financial system stress, making it a portfolio hedge against tail risks
  • **Diversifier**: Gold's low correlation to equities, bonds, and industrial commodities provides genuine portfolio diversification

    • The Relationship Between Gold and Critical Minerals

    Gold and critical minerals are not unrelated. Several connections link them:

  • **Co-production**: Many critical mineral deposits produce gold as a byproduct (Skaergaard produces both PGMs and gold; copper-gold deposits produce both metals)
  • **Economic enabler**: Gold revenue from byproduct production can make critical mineral projects economically viable, effectively subsidizing critical mineral supply
  • **Price cycles**: Gold and critical minerals may experience different price cycles, providing natural portfolio diversification within the mining sector
  • **Jurisdictional overlap**: The best mining jurisdictions for critical minerals are often the same as those for gold (Canada, Australia, Scandinavia)

    • Gold as a Portfolio Stabilizer

    Critical minerals investments tend to be volatile. Lithium prices have swung from US$10,000 to US$80,000+ per tonne and back within a few years. Rare earth prices show similar volatility. Gold, while not immune to price swings, has been less volatile than most critical minerals over the long term.

    Including gold in a critical minerals portfolio provides:

  • **Lower overall portfolio volatility**: Gold's more stable price trajectory reduces the volatility of a portfolio dominated by cyclical industrial commodities
  • **Liquidity**: Gold is the most liquid commodity, with deep, 24-hour global markets. This liquidity is valuable in a portfolio that includes less liquid critical mineral investments
  • **Downside protection**: During economic downturns or market corrections, gold's safe-haven demand provides a cushion against losses in more cyclical commodity positions

    • The Critical Minerals Act and Gold

    The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act and US critical mineral policy focus on minerals essential for clean energy, digital technologies, and defense. Gold is not explicitly included in most critical minerals lists, but the policy environment benefits gold indirectly:

  • **Mining investment**: Policies that encourage mining investment in Western jurisdictions benefit all mining activity, including gold
  • **Infrastructure**: Mining infrastructure (roads, ports, power) built for critical minerals projects can be shared with gold operations
  • **Workforce**: Mining skills developed for critical minerals projects are transferable to gold mining
  • **Permitting reform**: Streamlined permitting processes that accelerate critical minerals development also benefit gold projects

    • How to Include Gold in a Critical Minerals Portfolio

    Several approaches work for adding gold to a critical minerals strategy:

  • **Co-product producers**: Invest in mining companies that produce gold alongside critical minerals. Skaergaard (GRML) is a prime example, producing gold as a co-product with PGMs. The gold byproduct credit improves overall economics while providing gold price exposure
  • **Arctic gold**: Focus on gold production from geopolitically strategic Arctic regions (Greenland, Canada, Scandinavia) that align with Western supply diversification objectives
  • **Byproduct credits**: Evaluate mining companies partly on the strength of their byproduct gold credits, which reduce operating costs and provide earnings diversification

    Conclusion

    Gold deserves a place in critical minerals investment strategies. It provides portfolio stability, strategic hedging, and revenue diversification that pure critical mineral investments cannot match. For investors building a Western-aligned critical minerals portfolio, Skaergaard offers a unique combination of critical PGM exposure and gold byproduct credits, delivering both strategic and financial benefits in a single investment.
