Gold Supply Deficit and the Role of New Discoveries

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

gold supplydeficitnew discoveriesskaergaard

The global gold market is approaching a structural supply deficit. Mine production has plateaued, recycling is constrained, and demand from central banks, investors, and jewelry buyers continues to grow. This supply-demand imbalance has significant implications for gold prices and for the valuation of new gold development projects.

The Supply Picture

Global gold mine production has been relatively flat since 2018, averaging approximately 3,600-3,700 tonnes per year. This follows a period of growth from approximately 2,600 tonnes in 2010, driven primarily by expansions at existing mines and the development of new projects in emerging jurisdictions.

Several factors are constraining mine supply growth:

  • **Declining ore grades**: The average gold grade of producing mines has been declining for decades. Lower grades mean more ore must be processed to produce the same amount of gold, increasing costs and requiring larger operations
  • **Depletion of existing reserves**: Many of the world's largest gold mines are mature and depleting. Without replacement, global production will decline
  • **Permitting challenges**: New mine development faces increasingly lengthy and uncertain permitting processes in many jurisdictions
  • **Capital discipline**: Mining companies have been more disciplined with capital allocation since the post-2012 downturn, focusing on returns over growth
  • **Discovery drought**: The rate of new gold discoveries has been declining for over a decade. Major new deposits (>5 million ounces) are increasingly rare

    • The Demand Picture

    On the demand side, several structural factors support continued gold consumption:

  • **Central bank buying**: 1,000+ tonnes annually, a structural shift driven by de-dollarization and reserve diversification
  • **Jewelry**: The largest demand category at approximately 2,000+ tonnes annually, driven by India and China
  • **Investment**: Gold ETFs, bar and coin demand, and other investment vehicles
  • **Technology**: Gold's unique properties maintain demand from electronics, medicine, and other industries

    • Total gold demand has exceeded mine supply plus recycling in several recent years, drawing down above-ground stocks to fill the gap. This cannot continue indefinitely without upward price pressure.

    The Deficit Scenario

    If current trends continue, the gold market faces a growing supply deficit:

  • **Base case**: Mine supply remains flat at ~3,700 tonnes, recycling provides ~1,200 tonnes, total supply ~4,900 tonnes. Total demand ~5,000-5,200 tonnes, creating a 100-300 tonne annual deficit
  • **Bull case**: Mine supply declines at 1-2% per year due to depletion, recycling stagnates, demand grows with central bank buying. Annual deficit could reach 300-500 tonnes
  • **Bear case**: Gold price decline reduces demand, new projects come online, deficit narrows or closes

    • Under the base and bull cases, gold prices must rise to bring new supply online and reduce demand to balance the market.

    Where Will New Supply Come From?

    New gold supply can come from several sources:

  • **Greenfield discoveries**: New deposits like Skaergaard that are discovered and advanced through the development pipeline. This is the most important long-term source but also the slowest, with 10-15 year development timelines
  • **Brownfield expansions**: Expansions at existing mines that increase production capacity. These are faster to develop but constrained by existing deposit limits
  • **Low-grade projects**: Deposits that were uneconomic at lower prices become viable at higher prices. This is a significant source of marginal supply
  • **Byproduct gold**: Gold produced as a byproduct of copper, PGM, and other metal mining. Skaergaard falls into this category
  • **Recycling increase**: Higher gold prices incentivize more recycling, but the elasticity of recycling supply is limited

    • Skaergaard's Role in the Supply Picture

    Skaergaard's 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces, if developed, could contribute meaningfully to Western gold supply:

  • **Production potential**: Depending on mine plan and gold cut-off, Skaergaard could produce 100,000-300,000 ounces of gold per year as a byproduct
  • **Supply contribution**: This would represent 1-3% of global gold mine supply, not insignificant for a single project
  • **Western alignment**: As a Danish jurisdiction project, Skaergaard's gold supply would flow through Western-aligned supply chains
  • **Timeline**: If development proceeds on schedule, production could begin by 2030-2032, contributing to supply during a period of growing deficit

    • The Discovery Pipeline

    The gold exploration industry is not generating enough new discoveries to replace depleting reserves. Key metrics:

  • **Discovery cost**: The cost per ounce of new gold discovered has been rising for over a decade, driven by declining discovery rates and increasing exploration costs
  • **Discovery size**: The average size of new gold discoveries has been declining. The number of new deposits exceeding 5 million ounces has fallen significantly
  • **Discovery location**: New discoveries are increasingly in remote and challenging locations (Arctic, deep underground, politically complex jurisdictions)

    • This discovery drought means that deposits with Skaergaard's scale are increasingly valuable. Large, well-located, jurisdictionally secure gold resources are a scarce asset class.

    Investment Implications

    The supply deficit creates a favorable environment for gold development projects:

  • **Higher prices**: Deficit-driven price increases improve the economics of all gold projects
  • **Development incentive**: Higher prices incentivize companies to advance projects through the development pipeline
  • **Valuation premium**: Projects with development potential command a valuation premium in a supply-constrained market
  • **M&A activity**: Mining companies may acquire development-stage projects to replace depleting reserves, creating acquisition premiums

    • Conclusion

    The global gold market faces a structural supply deficit driven by flat mine production and growing demand. New deposits like Skaergaard are needed to fill the gap, and they are increasingly valuable in a supply-constrained market. Skaergaard's 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces represent a significant Western-aligned gold resource that could contribute 100,000-300,000 ounces of annual production. For investors, the supply deficit supports both higher gold prices and higher valuations for gold development projects.
