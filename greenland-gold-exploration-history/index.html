Greenland Gold Exploration History: From Early Prospecting to Modern Discovery

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

Gold has been part of Greenland's geological story for millennia, but systematic exploration for economic gold deposits is a relatively modern chapter in the territory's history. Understanding this exploration trajectory provides context for evaluating the significance of discoveries like Skaergaard and the future potential of Greenland's gold sector.

Early Gold in Greenland

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Gold occurrences in Greenland have been known for centuries, primarily through alluvial gold found in river sediments. Indigenous Greenlandic populations used gold for decorative purposes long before European geological surveyors documented the mineral's presence. The first recorded geological observations of gold in Greenland date to the 19th century, when Danish and Norwegian geologists noted gold mineralization in several locations.

However, these early observations did not lead to serious exploration. Greenland's remoteness, harsh climate, and limited infrastructure made mining impractical for most of the 20th century. Gold exploration remained sporadic, driven primarily by academic interest rather than commercial motivation.

The 1980s-1990s Exploration Wave

The modern era of gold exploration in Greenland began in the 1980s and accelerated in the 1990s, driven by:

  • Rising gold prices: Gold prices increased significantly in the late 1970s and 1980s, making remote exploration economically attractive
  • Improved technology: Advances in geophysical surveying and geochemical sampling made Arctic exploration more efficient
  • Government incentives: The Danish and Greenlandic governments began promoting mineral exploration as a path to economic development
  • Geological understanding: Systematic geological mapping by the Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS) provided a framework for targeting exploration

During this period, several companies conducted exploration programs across southern and western Greenland, identifying gold occurrences in the Ketilidian mobile belt, the Rinkian belt, and other geological provinces.

Nalunaq: Greenland's First Gold Mine

The most significant result of this exploration wave was the Nalunaq gold mine in South Greenland:

  • Discovery: Gold mineralization at Nalunaq was identified in the early 1990s by Greenlandic geologists
  • Development: The mine was developed by Crew Gold Corporation and achieved commercial production in 2004
  • Production: Nalunaq produced approximately 350,000 ounces of gold between 2004 and 2013, with peak production of approximately 55,000 ounces per year
  • Operations: Underground mining operation with processing through a gravity-flotation circuit
  • Closure: Mining was suspended in 2013 due to declining gold prices, geological complexity, and operational challenges
  • Legacy: Nalunaq demonstrated that gold mining is technically feasible in Greenland, but also highlighted the challenges of Arctic operations (remote location, high costs, narrow vein geometry)

Post-Nalunaq Activity

After Nalunaq's closure, gold exploration in Greenland continued but at a reduced pace. Several companies maintained exploration programs:

  • Aappaluttoq/Greenland Gold: Subsequent owners explored restart options for Nalunaq, conducting additional drilling and metallurgical testing
  • NunaMinerals: A Greenlandic exploration company that conducted regional gold exploration programs
  • Hudson Resources: Explored various mineral targets in West Greenland, including gold
  • Angus Mackirk: Conducted alluvial gold exploration in southern Greenland

None of these programs resulted in a new gold mine, but they contributed to Greenland's geological knowledge base and identified additional gold targets for future exploration.

Skaergaard: A New Chapter

The discovery and delineation of Skaergaard's gold resource (23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces) represents a step-change in Greenland's gold potential:

  • Scale: Skaergaard's gold endowment is orders of magnitude larger than any previous Greenland gold discovery
  • Location: East Greenland, a region with limited prior exploration compared to the south and west
  • Co-product status: Gold is recovered alongside palladium, improving overall project economics
  • Strategic timing: The discovery comes at a time of elevated gold prices and growing Western interest in critical mineral supply diversification

Skaergaard has the potential to make Greenland a significant gold-producing jurisdiction for the first time.

Current Exploration Landscape

Greenland's gold exploration landscape in 2026 is shaped by several factors:

  • Improved accessibility: Climate change has extended the exploration season in some areas, though this creates environmental complications
  • Government support: Greenland's pro-mining policy and regulatory reform encourage exploration investment
  • Strategic interest: Western government interest in Arctic mineral supply creates policy tailwinds for gold exploration
  • Technology advances: Improved drilling technology, geophysical methods, and data analytics make remote exploration more efficient

Several companies are actively exploring for gold across Greenland, though the pace of activity remains modest compared to more established mining jurisdictions.

Future Potential

Greenland's geological potential for gold remains underexplored. The territory covers 2.16 million square kilometers, much of which has seen only reconnaissance-level exploration. Key areas of future gold potential include:

  • Ketilidian mobile belt: Historically the most productive gold region in Greenland, with numerous gold occurrences
  • Nagssugtoqidian orogen: Underexplored but geologically prospective for orogenic gold deposits
  • East Greenland: Skaergaard has demonstrated the potential of this underexplored region
  • North Greenland: Largely unexplored due to extreme remoteness but geologically interesting

Conclusion

Greenland's gold exploration history is relatively short but increasingly significant. From early alluvial discoveries through the Nalunaq mine to the Skaergaard resource, each chapter has added to the understanding of Greenland's gold potential. Skaergaard represents the most important gold discovery in Greenland's history and may mark the beginning of a new era of gold production from the Arctic. The exploration frontier in Greenland remains vast, and future discoveries could further transform the territory's role in global gold supply.
