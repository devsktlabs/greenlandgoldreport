Gold Prices at $5,100: What This Means for Greenland Mining

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

gold pricegreenland mininginvestmentgrmleconomics

Gold at US$5,100 per ounce (Feb 2026) represents a historic high that has transformed the economics of gold-bearing mining projects in Greenland and across the Arctic. The sustained high-price environment has fundamentally changed the economics of projects that were marginal at lower price levels and created new interest in exploration and development.

The Price Environment

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Gold first broke above US$2,000 per ounce in late 2023, surged past US$3,000 in 2025, and reached US$5,100 by February 2026. Key factors supporting the price:

  • **Central bank buying**: Over 1,000 tonnes of official sector purchases annually absorbing physical supply
  • **Geopolitical tensions**: Ongoing conflicts and international friction support safe-haven demand
  • **Interest rate environment**: While rates have increased, real rates in many economies remain negative after inflation, supporting gold's zero-yield characteristics
  • **De-dollarization trends**: Some nations and institutions reducing dollar exposure in favor of gold
  • **Physical demand**: Strong jewelry and bar-and-coin demand, particularly from India and China

    • Why $5,100 Changes Everything for Greenland

    The difference between a US$1,200 gold price and a US$5,100 gold price (Feb 2026) is transformative for Arctic mining economics:

  • **Breakeven reduction**: Many Arctic gold deposits that were uneconomic at US$1,200/oz become viable at US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026), as the higher revenue covers higher Arctic operating costs
  • **Margin expansion**: For deposits that were already economic, the additional US$3,900/oz per ounce above the prior US$1,200 baseline flows largely to the bottom line, significantly improving project returns
  • **Investment case strengthening**: Higher gold prices make the investment case for Arctic development more compelling to both equity investors and project finance lenders
  • **Exploration incentive**: Higher prices incentivize exploration spending, potentially leading to new discoveries

    • Skaergaard Economics at Different Gold Prices

    The sensitivity of Skaergaard's project economics to gold prices illustrates the impact:

  • **At US$1,500/oz gold**: Gold credits provide a modest cost offset; project economics rely primarily on palladium
  • **At US$5,100/oz gold (Feb 2026)**: Gold credits provide a significant cost offset, reducing effective PGM AISC by US$150-300/oz
  • **At US$2,500/oz gold**: Gold credits could reduce effective PGM AISC by US$250-400/oz, making Skaergaard highly competitive even in a lower palladium price environment

    • The gold price sensitivity means that Skaergaard's economics improve continuously as gold rises, providing a natural hedge against palladium price weakness.

    Nalunaq Restart Economics

    The Nalunaq gold mine in South Greenland, which closed in 2013 when gold prices were below US$1,300/oz, is frequently discussed as a potential restart candidate. At current gold prices above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026), the economics are substantially different:

  • **Operating costs**: Nalunaq's operating costs were estimated at US$1,200-1,300/oz when it closed. While costs have likely increased due to inflation, gold prices have risen more
  • **Grade**: Higher-grade ore could be targeted in a restart, further improving economics
  • **Infrastructure**: Existing decline, tailings facility, and surface infrastructure reduce restart capital requirements

    • Nalunaq's restart potential demonstrates how the gold price environment has changed the economics of Greenland mining. Projects that were marginal or uneconomic five years ago are now viable.

    New Exploration Incentives

    Higher gold prices incentivize exploration activity across Greenland:

  • **Risk tolerance**: Higher prices justify spending on riskier, more remote exploration targets
  • **Financing availability**: Exploration companies find it easier to raise capital when the commodity price environment is favorable
  • **Junior company activity**: Small exploration companies, which are the primary drivers of grassroots exploration, are more active in favorable price environments

    • This exploration activity could lead to new gold discoveries in Greenland beyond Skaergaard, potentially creating additional development opportunities.

    Currency and Cost Considerations

    Gold is priced in US dollars, while many Arctic mining costs are incurred in local currencies (Danish krone for Greenland). The relationship between the US dollar and local currencies affects profitability:

  • **Strong dollar**: A strong US dollar means gold revenues (in dollars) convert to more local currency, covering more costs
  • **Weak dollar**: A weak dollar reduces the local currency value of gold revenues

    • The current dollar environment has been generally supportive for gold mining economics in non-dollar jurisdictions.

    Downside Risk: What If Gold Falls?

    Gold prices are not guaranteed to remain above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026). Potential downside catalysts include:

  • **Central bank selling**: If major central banks reverse their buying stance and begin selling gold reserves
  • **Resolution of geopolitical tensions**: Significant de-escalation could reduce safe-haven demand
  • **Rising real interest rates**: If real rates increase significantly, gold becomes less attractive relative to yield-bearing assets

    • However, the structural demand from central bank buying provides a more robust price floor than existed before 2022. A return to US$1,200/oz gold seems unlikely in the current environment.

    Conclusion

    Gold above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026) has transformed the economics of Arctic and Greenland mining. Skaergaard benefits directly through enhanced gold byproduct credits that reduce net PGM production costs. The favorable price environment also improves the investment case for Nalunaq restart and incentivizes new gold exploration across Greenland. For gold-focused investors, the current price environment makes Arctic gold supply development more attractive than at any point in the past decade.
