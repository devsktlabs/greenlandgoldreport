How Gold Byproduct Credits Improve PGM Project Economics

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

gold byproductpgm economicsskaergaardmining finance

In mining finance, byproduct credits are one of the most powerful yet underappreciated factors in project economics. When a mine produces more than one metal, the revenue from secondary (byproduct) metals directly offsets the cost of producing the primary metal. For PGM deposits with gold content, this mechanism can be transformative.

The Mechanics of Byproduct Credits

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

The concept is simple. A mining operation incurs costs to extract and process ore. These costs include:

  • Mining (drilling, blasting, loading, hauling)
  • Processing (crushing, grinding, flotation, dewatering)
  • Overhead (administration, permitting, community relations)
  • Sustaining capital (equipment replacement, tailings management)

If the ore contains both palladium and gold, the revenue from gold sales directly offsets the total cost of production. The remaining cost, after gold credits, is the net cost attributable to palladium production.

Example: If total costs are US$50 per tonne of ore processed, and the ore produces 2 grams of palladium (worth US$1.90) and 1 gram of gold (worth US$65.00), the gold credit of US$65 per tonne exceeds the total mining cost, making the palladium effectively free to produce. While this is a simplified example, it illustrates how significant gold credits can be.

Gold's Outsize Impact

Gold commands a high per-ounce price relative to most byproduct metals:

  • Gold price: ~US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026)
  • Palladium price: ~US$950/oz
  • Silver price: ~US$25/oz
  • Copper price: ~US$4.00/lb (~US$8,800/tonne)

This means that even modest gold grades in the ore generate significant per-tonne revenue. A deposit that grades 0.5 grams per tonne gold generates approximately US$32 per tonne in gold revenue, which can represent a meaningful fraction of total mining and processing costs.

Skaergaard's Gold Credit

At Skaergaard, the gold credit is particularly impactful:

  • The resource contains 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces alongside 25.4 million palladium-equivalent ounces
  • The gold-to-palladium ratio in the resource means gold contributes a significant share of total metal value
  • At US$5,100/oz gold (Feb 2026) and US$950/oz palladium, gold may account for 30-40% of total metal revenue despite being the "byproduct"

The practical impact is a reduction in effective palladium AISC of US$150-300 per ounce, depending on the specific gold grade in the processing feed.

Comparison with PGM-Only Projects

The difference between PGM-gold projects and PGM-only projects is significant:

PGM-gold project (like Skaergaard):

  • Higher total revenue per tonne of ore
  • Lower net PGM cost after gold credits
  • Broader investor appeal (gold and PGM investors)
  • Diversified revenue reduces sensitivity to any single metal price

PGM-only project:

  • Revenue depends entirely on PGM prices
  • Higher sensitivity to PGM price declines
  • Narrower investor base
  • No hedge against PGM-specific market disruptions

Sensitivity to Gold Prices

The byproduct credit mechanism means that Skaergaard's economics are positively leveraged to gold prices:

  • Gold at US$2,500/oz: Additional US$50-75 per ounce credit improvement
  • Gold at US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026): Current base case
  • Gold at US$1,500/oz: Credit reduction of US$75-100 per ounce
  • Gold at US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026): Significant reduction in economics, making project more dependent on palladium price

The current gold price environment above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026) maximizes the byproduct credit benefit. Even at lower gold prices, the credit remains meaningful.

Investor Implications

For investors evaluating Skaergaard against PGM-only competitors:

  • Look at net costs, not gross costs: Compare effective AISC after all byproduct credits, not headline mining costs
  • Model gold price scenarios: Project economics should be tested at conservative gold prices (US$1,500/oz) to understand downside risk
  • Value the optionality: The gold credit provides optionality that PGM-only projects cannot match. In a rising gold environment, this optionality has real value

Conclusion

Gold byproduct credits are a material factor in Skaergaard's project economics. They reduce effective palladium production costs, broaden investor appeal, and provide a natural hedge against palladium price weakness. In the current high-gold-price environment, the credit is particularly valuable. Investors should evaluate Skaergaard on an after-credit basis to fully appreciate the project's competitive position relative to PGM-only alternatives.
