Greenland Gold Permitting Pathway: From Exploration to Production

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

greenlandpermittingregulationmining law

Greenland's mining regulatory framework is relatively young but increasingly well-defined. Understanding the permitting pathway from exploration to gold production is essential for investors evaluating development timelines and risks for projects like Skaergaard.

The Regulatory Framework

Greenland's mining sector is governed primarily by:

  • Mineral Resources Act (Mineralloven): The overarching law governing mineral exploration and exploitation in Greenland. Administered by the Government of Greenland through the Mineral Resources Authority
  • Subsidence Act: Governs compensation for damage caused by mining activities
  • Environmental Protection Act: Sets environmental standards and assessment requirements
  • Nature Protection Act: Protects designated natural areas and species
  • Building Regulations: Governs construction permits for mining infrastructure
  • Radiation Protection Act: Relevant for gold projects with naturally occurring radioactive materials

Greenland took over responsibility for mineral resources from Denmark in 2009 as part of the Self-Rule Act, and the Mineral Resources Act was subsequently enacted.

The Permitting Pathway

Stage 1: Exploration License

An exploration license grants the holder exclusive rights to explore for minerals within a defined area.

  • Application: Submitted to the Mineral Resources Authority, including proposed exploration area, work program, and environmental baseline plan
  • Term: Typically 5 years, renewable for additional 5-year periods
  • Requirements: Annual work expenditure commitments, environmental impact reporting, and community consultation
  • Size limits: Maximum area limits apply (typically 500-1,000 square kilometers per license)
  • Number of licenses: A single company may hold multiple exploration licenses

Stage 2: Exploitation (Production) License

An exploitation license grants the holder the right to commercially extract minerals. This is the key permit for production.

  • Application: Requires a comprehensive exploitation plan including mine design, processing, waste management, environmental impact assessment, social impact assessment, and closure plan
  • Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA): A full EIA is required, typically taking 1-3 years to complete. Includes baseline environmental studies, impact prediction, mitigation planning, and public consultation
  • Strategic Environmental Assessment (SEA): For large projects, a strategic-level environmental assessment may also be required
  • Public consultation: Public hearings and comment periods are required as part of the EIA process
  • Decision: The Government of Greenland makes the final permitting decision, typically taking 6-12 months after EIA completion
  • Term: Typically 30 years or the life of the mine, whichever is shorter, with extension options

Stage 3: Construction and Commissioning

After receiving an exploitation license:

  • Construction permits: Additional permits required for specific construction activities (port, roads, processing plant, camps)
  • Water use permits: Permits for water intake and discharge
  • Waste management permits: Permits for tailings storage, waste rock disposal, and other waste streams
  • Construction: Building mining infrastructure, typically 2-4 years for a new Arctic mine
  • Commissioning: Testing and ramping up processing operations, typically 6-12 months

Stage 4: Operations

During mining operations:

  • Annual reporting: Environmental monitoring reports, production reports, and financial reports submitted to regulatory authorities
  • Compliance monitoring: Regular inspections by regulatory agencies
  • Social engagement: Ongoing community consultation and benefit agreement compliance
  • Royalties and taxes: Payment of royalties (typically 2.5-5% of revenue) and corporate taxes

Stage 5: Closure

After mining ceases:

  • Closure plan implementation: Decommissioning infrastructure, reclaiming disturbed land, and establishing post-closure land use
  • Financial assurance: Release of financial assurance (bonding) contingent on successful reclamation
  • Post-closure monitoring: Ongoing environmental monitoring, potentially for decades

Timeline for Skaergaard

Based on the Greenland permitting pathway, Skaergaard's development timeline would approximately be:

  • Exploration (completed): Resource definition drilling and metallurgical testing
  • Pre-feasibility study: Ongoing or near completion
  • EIA commencement: 2026-2027
  • EIA completion: 2027-2029
  • Exploitation license application: 2029
  • Permitting decision: 2029-2030
  • Construction: 2030-2032
  • Commissioning: 2032
  • Production: 2033+

This timeline is approximate and depends on the pace of EIA completion, government decision-making, and construction.

Permitting Risks

Founded and led by CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML) controls the Skaergaard project in southeast Greenland.

Exploration License Risk: GRML holds three Mineral Exploration Licenses (MELs) totaling 877 km². MEL 2012-25 (Sødalen camp and airstrip, 16 km²) and MEL 2021-10 (754 km² exploration area) both expire December 31, 2026. MEL 2007-01 (107 km², hosts the Skaergaard Intrusion) remains active until December 31, 2027. These renewals represent a material near-term risk factor.

Key permitting risks for gold projects in Greenland include:

  • EIA delays: Baseline data collection and impact assessment can take longer than planned, particularly if environmental complexity is greater than anticipated
  • Political risk: Changes in government or policy could affect permitting timelines or requirements
  • Community opposition: Local community opposition could delay or prevent permit approval
  • Environmental concerns: Unforeseen environmental issues identified during the EIA could require additional mitigation measures or design changes
  • Uranium policy: Projects with uranium content face additional regulatory complexity due to Greenland's uranium policy (relevant for projects like Kvanefjeld)

> Note: Kvanefjeld is a rare earth-uranium project owned by Greenland Minerals Limited (ASX: GGG), a completely separate company from Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML).

Conclusion

Greenland's permitting pathway is well-defined but can be lengthy. The EIA process is the most time-consuming step, typically requiring 1-3 years. For investors evaluating Skaergaard, the permitting timeline is a key factor in project risk and return expectations. Greenland's regulatory framework provides transparency and predictability, but the Arctic environment's complexity adds to the time required for thorough environmental assessment. Understanding the permitting pathway is essential for making informed investment decisions about Greenland gold projects.
