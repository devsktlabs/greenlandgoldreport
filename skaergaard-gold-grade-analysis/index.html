Skaergaard Gold Grade Analysis: Distribution and Economic Impact

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

skaergaardgold graderesource analysiseconomics

Understanding the gold grade distribution at Skaergaard is essential for evaluating the project's economics and optimizing mine plans. Gold at Skaergaard is not uniformly distributed. It occurs in enriched zones within the broader mineralized intrusion, and the spatial variability of gold grades has direct implications for mining sequence, processing strategy, and cash flow timing.

Gold Grade Distribution at Skaergaard

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

The Skaergaard intrusion is a layered mafic body that hosts PGM and gold mineralization in specific horizons. Key characteristics of gold grade distribution:

  • Layered controls: Gold mineralization is concentrated in specific layers of the intrusion, particularly in the Triple Group and the Upper Border Group where sulfide mineralization is most abundant
  • Lateral continuity: Gold-bearing zones show good lateral continuity along strike, allowing for consistent mining plans within individual zones
  • Vertical variation: Gold grade varies with depth, with some zones showing enrichment at depth while others are more consistent
  • Nugget effect: Gold at Skaergaard includes both fine-grained (associated with sulfides) and coarser-grained (free gold) occurrences, creating sampling variability that is typical of gold deposits

Gold Grade by Zone

While the exact grade distribution depends on the specific resource model and cut-off grade applied, the general pattern at Skaergaard is:

  • PGM-dominant zones: Highest palladium grades with moderate gold grades. These zones are the primary economic drivers for the project
  • Gold-enriched zones: Specific horizons where gold grades are elevated relative to the deposit average. These zones contribute disproportionately to gold revenue
  • Mixed zones: Zones where PGM and gold grades are both moderate, contributing to overall metal production without extreme values in either

The identification and characterization of gold-enriched zones is critical for mine planning, as these zones can be targeted during specific mining periods to maximize gold revenue.

Cut-off Grade Considerations

The cut-off grade is the minimum grade above which ore is processed and below which waste is sent to the dump. For multi-metal deposits like Skaergaard, the cut-off grade is typically expressed as a net smelter return (NSR) cut-off, which accounts for the combined value of all recovered metals:

  • Higher cut-off: Results in higher average processed grades and lower total ounces recovered, maximizing short-term profitability
  • Lower cut-off: Results in lower average grades but higher total ounces recovered, maximizing life-of-mine production
  • Optimal cut-off: Balances grade, tonnage, and timing to maximize net present value

The gold price directly affects the optimal cut-off grade. Higher gold prices lower the effective cut-off, making more ore economic to process. At US$5,100/oz gold (Feb 2026), the lower cut-off allows more gold-bearing ore to be processed than at US$1,500/oz, increasing total gold production.

Gold Recovery

Gold recovery at Skaergaard through flotation processing is expected to be high, though not perfect:

  • Flotation recovery: Sulfide-associated gold is recoverable through flotation with recovery rates of 85-95%, depending on the specific mineralogy and grind size
  • Gravity recovery: Coarse free gold may be recoverable through gravity concentration before flotation, potentially adding 5-10% recovery above flotation alone
  • Overall recovery: Combined flotation and gravity recovery is expected to achieve 90-95% overall gold recovery

Metallurgical test work on representative ore samples is essential to confirm recovery assumptions. Variability in gold mineralization style (fine-grained versus coarse-grained) across the deposit means recovery may vary by mining zone.

Economic Impact of Gold Grade Variability

The variability of gold grades across Skaergaard has several economic implications:

  • Cash flow timing: Mining higher-gold zones early in the mine life generates more gold revenue in the early years, improving project economics and reducing payback period
  • Grade risk: If actual mined grades are lower than resource model predictions, gold revenue and project returns are reduced
  • Processing flexibility: The ability to blend ores from different zones with different gold grades provides processing flexibility and risk management
  • Gold price leverage: Higher gold grades increase the project's leverage to gold prices, making returns more sensitive to gold price changes

Comparison with Other Multi-Metal Deposits

Skaergaard's gold grade profile can be compared to other multi-metal deposits:

  • Platreef (Ivanhoe Mines, South Africa): PGE-nickel-copper deposit with gold as a byproduct. Gold grades are typically lower than Skaergaard's, but the deposit is very large
  • Munali (Sable Metals, Zambia): Nickel sulfide deposit with PGM and gold credits. Gold grades are modest but contribute to economics
  • Sudbury Basin (multiple operators, Canada): Nickel-copper-PGM deposits with gold as a byproduct. Gold grades vary but generally support meaningful byproduct credits

Skaergaard's gold grades are competitive with these comparable deposits, supporting the conclusion that gold credits are a material economic factor.

Grade Control and Reconciliation

During mining operations, grade control and reconciliation are essential to ensure that actual mined grades match resource model predictions:

  • Blast-hole sampling: Sampling of blast holes provides near-real-time grade information for ore classification
  • Ore sorting: Advanced ore sorting technology can separate higher-grade from lower-grade ore before processing
  • Mine-to-mill reconciliation: Comparing predicted grades (from the resource model) with actual processed grades (from the mill) to validate the model and adjust future predictions

Conclusion

Gold grade distribution at Skaergaard is a critical factor in project economics. The layered nature of the deposit creates enriched gold zones that can be targeted for maximum gold revenue. Cut-off grade optimization, recovery assumptions, and grade control are all important considerations. For investors, the gold grade profile adds optionality and upside to Skaergaard's investment case, particularly in a high-gold-price environment where gold credits become increasingly valuable.
