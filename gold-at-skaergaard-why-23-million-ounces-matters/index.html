Gold at Skaergaard: Why 23 Million Ounces Changes the Equation

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

skaergaardgoldgrmlgreenland goldbyproduct

When investors hear "Skaergaard," they think palladium. That makes sense given the deposit is palladium-dominant, but the gold story at Skaergaard deserves equal attention. With 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces in the indicated resource, Skaergaard would rank among the significant gold deposits in the Arctic and sub-Arctic regions even without its PGM content.

The Scale of the Gold Resource

To put 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces in context:

  • **Kittila mine (Finland)**: One of Europe's largest gold mines, with reserves of approximately 4.5 million ounces
  • **Fekola mine (Mali)**: A major West African gold mine with reserves of approximately 6 million ounces
  • **Canadian Malartic (Quebec)**: Canada's largest gold mine by production, with reserves of approximately 10 million ounces

    • Skaergaard's gold endowment exceeds all of these on a resource basis. While the gold is not the primary product and is reported as "equivalent" ounces (reflecting co-product status with palladium), the in-situ gold value at US$2,000 per ounce exceeds US$47 billion.

    This gold is not a minor byproduct. It represents a significant share of Skaergaard's total value and provides investment exposure to gold that attracts a broader investor base than PGM-focused mining companies typically reach.

    Gold Grade and Distribution

    The gold at Skaergaard is distributed throughout the mineralized zones, with higher concentrations in specific layers of the layered mafic intrusion. Key characteristics:

  • **Total gold resource**: 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces (indicated category)
  • **Grade**: Variable across mineralized zones, with enriched intervals containing multi-gram per tonne gold grades
  • **Mineralization style**: Gold is associated with sulfide-rich layers, often occurring as free gold or gold associated with base metal sulfides
  • **Recovery**: Gold is recoverable through standard flotation processing, with additional gravity recovery potential for coarser gold particles

    • The gold grade distribution across the deposit means that mining can be optimized to target higher-gold zones during certain periods, providing flexibility to respond to gold price movements.

    How Gold Credits Improve PGM Economics

    The gold byproduct credit is one of Skaergaard's most important economic advantages. Here is how it works:

  • **Mining cost allocation**: Total mining and processing costs are allocated across all recovered metals. Gold revenue offsets a portion of these costs, reducing the net cost attributable to palladium production
  • **Effective AISC reduction**: At a gold price of US$2,000/oz, gold credits could reduce Skaergaard's effective PGM all-in sustaining cost by US$150-300 per ounce
  • **Breakeven sensitivity**: The breakeven palladium price (the price at which Skaergaard covers all costs) is significantly lower than for PGM-only projects thanks to gold credits

    • This cost offset is not trivial. In a market where PGM mining costs are rising across the industry due to deepening South African mines and energy inflation, any cost advantage is valuable.

    The Gold Price Tailwind

    Gold prices above US$2,000 per ounce provide a favorable environment for Skaergaard's gold economics:

  • **Central bank buying**: Central banks purchased 1,045 tonnes of gold in 2024, a near-record pace driven by diversification away from US dollar reserves
  • **Geopolitical uncertainty**: Ongoing geopolitical tensions support gold as a safe-haven asset
  • **Inflation hedge**: Gold's role as an inflation hedge remains relevant despite declining headline inflation in some economies
  • **Supply constraints**: Gold mine supply has been relatively flat, supporting prices

    • If gold prices remain elevated or continue to rise, the gold credit at Skaergaard becomes increasingly valuable, improving project economics beyond current estimates.

    Greenland Gold Context

    Greenland has a history of gold exploration and production that provides context for Skaergaard's gold endowment:

  • **Nalunaq**: Greenland's only gold mine to date, producing approximately 350,000 ounces between 2004 and 2013
  • **Exploration history**: Multiple companies have explored for gold across Greenland since the 1990s, identifying numerous gold occurrences but few deposits approaching Skaergaard's scale
  • **Geological potential**: Greenland's geology includes several gold-bearing terrains, including the Ketilidian mobile belt in South Greenland and the Nagssugtoqidian orogen in West Greenland

    • Skaergaard's 23.5 million ounces dwarfs every other gold occurrence identified in Greenland, making it the most significant gold discovery in the territory's history.

    Investor Appeal

    Gold exposure broadens Skaergaard's investor appeal:

  • **Gold-focused funds**: Some institutional funds specifically target gold exposure and would not invest in a PGM-only company
  • **Retail gold investors**: Individual investors who buy gold stocks as inflation hedges or safe-haven plays may find Skaergaard attractive
  • **Diversification**: The gold component provides portfolio diversification that pure PGM companies cannot match

    • Conclusion

    The gold at Skaergaard is not a footnote. It is a material asset that significantly improves project economics, broadens investor appeal, and provides a hedge against palladium-specific market risks. At 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces, Skaergaard is one of the largest gold resources in the Arctic, and the gold byproduct credit could be the difference between a marginal and a highly profitable mining operation.
