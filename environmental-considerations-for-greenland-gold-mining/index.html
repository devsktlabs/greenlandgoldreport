Environmental Considerations for Greenland Gold Mining

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

environmentgreenland goldpermittingsustainability

Greenland's Arctic environment is among the most pristine and ecologically sensitive on Earth. Gold mining in this environment requires careful environmental planning, rigorous impact assessment, and ongoing environmental management throughout the mine life and beyond. For projects like Skaergaard, environmental stewardship is not just a regulatory requirement but a critical factor in maintaining social license and attracting responsible investment.

The Greenland Environmental Framework

Greenland's environmental governance is shaped by its status within the Kingdom of Denmark and its unique Arctic ecology:

  • **Mineral Resources Act**: Greenland's primary mining law, administered by the Mineral Resources Authority, includes environmental impact assessment requirements
  • **Environmental Protection Act**: Provides the overarching framework for environmental regulation, including waste management, water quality, and biodiversity protection
  • **Nature Protection Act**: Protects designated natural areas and species of conservation concern
  • **International obligations**: Greenland, through Denmark, is party to international environmental agreements including the Arctic Council, OSPAR (marine protection), and CITES (species trade)
  • **EU influence**: Despite not being an EU member, Greenland's environmental standards are influenced by Danish and European regulatory traditions

    • Environmental Impact Assessment

    An environmental impact assessment (EIA) is required before any mining project can receive exploitation (production) permits. The EIA process in Greenland typically includes:

  • **Scoping**: Defining the scope of the environmental assessment, including spatial boundaries, environmental components to be assessed, and assessment methodology
  • **Baseline studies**: Comprehensive studies of the existing environment, including water quality, air quality, wildlife populations, vegetation, cultural heritage, and socioeconomic conditions. Baseline studies typically require 1-2 years of data collection to account for seasonal variability
  • **Impact prediction**: Predicting the environmental impacts of the proposed project, including direct impacts from mining and processing, indirect impacts from infrastructure development, and cumulative impacts from the project combined with other activities
  • **Mitigation planning**: Identifying measures to avoid, minimize, or compensate for predicted environmental impacts
  • **Monitoring plan**: Establishing an environmental monitoring program to verify impact predictions and assess the effectiveness of mitigation measures

    • Key Environmental Concerns for Gold Mining

    Water quality: Gold mining operations affect water quality through:

  • Tailings discharge containing residual chemicals from processing (cyanide in some operations, though Skaergaard's flotation process uses different reagents)
  • Acid rock drainage from sulfide-bearing waste rock
  • Sedimentation from construction and mining activities
  • Changes in groundwater levels and flow patterns

    • Greenland's streams, lakes, and coastal waters support fish populations and are used by local communities for subsistence fishing. Maintaining water quality is a critical environmental objective.

    Wildlife and habitat: Mining operations affect wildlife through:

  • Habitat loss and fragmentation from open-pit development, waste rock storage, and infrastructure
  • Disturbance from noise, dust, and vehicle traffic
  • Changes in water quality affecting aquatic habitats
  • Barriers to wildlife movement (roads, facilities)
  • Attraction of wildlife to mining areas (waste disposal, artificial water bodies)

    • Key species of concern include caribou/muskox, Arctic fox, polar bear (coastal areas), migratory birds, and marine mammals.

    Permafrost and terrain: Mining in permafrost regions causes terrain disturbances including:

  • Thaw subsidence from permafrost degradation
  • Changes in hydrology (surface water and groundwater flow patterns)
  • Terrain instability affecting roads, buildings, and waste storage facilities

    • Air quality: Dust from mining operations, emissions from diesel-powered equipment, and greenhouse gas emissions from energy consumption affect local and regional air quality.

    Cultural heritage: Greenland's coastal and inland areas contain archaeological sites, including Thule culture winter houses, Norse ruins, and more recent historical sites. Mining must avoid or mitigate impacts on cultural heritage.

    Closure and Post-Closure

    Environmental responsibility extends beyond mine operation to closure and post-closure:

  • **Mine closure plan**: Detailed plan for decommissioning mining infrastructure, reclaiming disturbed land, and establishing post-closure land use
  • **Tailings management**: Long-term management of tailings storage facilities to ensure physical stability and water quality protection
  • **Water treatment**: Ongoing water treatment may be required in perpetuity if acid rock drainage or other water quality issues persist
  • **Monitoring**: Environmental monitoring continues after closure to verify reclamation success and address any unforeseen impacts
  • **Financial assurance**: Mining companies must provide financial assurance (bonding) sufficient to cover closure and reclamation costs

    • Greenland's Competitive Advantage

    Greenland's environmental standards, while stringent, provide a competitive advantage for responsible mining:

  • **Social license**: Rigorous environmental review builds community and stakeholder confidence in the project
  • **Investor confidence**: ESG-conscious investors prefer projects in jurisdictions with strong environmental governance
  • **Brand value**: Gold produced in an environmentally responsible manner commands premium positioning in the marketplace
  • **Regulatory certainty**: Clear and predictable environmental rules reduce regulatory risk

    • Conclusion

    Environmental stewardship is a non-negotiable requirement for gold mining in Greenland. The EIA process, ongoing environmental management, and closure planning must be rigorous and transparent. For Skaergaard, environmental responsibility is an integral part of project development that directly affects social license, investor confidence, and ultimately project success. Greenland's strong environmental governance is an asset that positions the territory as a responsible source of Arctic gold supply.
