Central Bank Gold Buying and the Case for Arctic Gold Supply

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

Central banks have been buying gold at a pace not seen in decades. This structural demand driver has significant implications for gold prices and, by extension, for projects like Skaergaard that contribute gold to the global supply chain.

The Scale of Central Bank Buying

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Central bank gold purchases in 2024 reached approximately 1,045 tonnes, according to the World Gold Council. This was among the highest annual totals on record and continued a trend of sustained official sector gold accumulation that began in earnest around 2022.

The largest buyers have included:

  • People's Bank of China: The most significant buyer by volume, with official reserves increasing by several hundred tonnes
  • Reserve Bank of India: A consistent buyer adding to India's official gold holdings
  • Polish central bank: Among the most active European buyers
  • Central banks of Turkey, Singapore, and various Middle Eastern nations: Contributing to the aggregate demand

The driver behind this buying is straightforward: central banks are diversifying reserve portfolios away from US dollar-denominated assets. Geopolitical tensions, the weaponization of the dollar through sanctions, and a desire for reserve diversification have made gold an attractive alternative.

Why This Matters for Gold Supply

Central bank buying absorbs physical gold from the market without corresponding selling pressure. Unlike investment demand (which can reverse quickly), official sector gold purchases are typically long-term holdings that are unlikely to be sold except under extreme circumstances.

This creates a structural supply-demand imbalance:

  • Mine supply: Global gold mine production has been relatively flat at approximately 3,600-3,700 tonnes per year
  • Recycled supply: Gold recycling contributes approximately 1,100-1,300 tonnes annually, depending on gold prices
  • Total supply: Approximately 4,700-5,000 tonnes per year
  • Central bank demand alone: 1,000+ tonnes annually, representing 20%+ of total supply

When jewelry demand, technology demand, and investment demand are added to central bank buying, the total demand picture can exceed supply in certain periods, creating upward pressure on prices.

Implications for Gold Prices

The sustained level of central bank buying provides a price floor for gold that is more robust than at any point in the past decade. Key implications:

  • Higher equilibrium price: With central banks absorbing 20%+ of annual supply, the equilibrium gold price is higher than it would be without official sector demand
  • Price resilience: Gold prices have shown resilience to interest rate increases and dollar strength that would normally pressure gold lower, suggesting central bank buying is a dominant demand factor
  • Reduced downside risk: The structural demand from central banks makes a sustained gold price decline less likely

The Arctic Supply Opportunity

New gold supply is needed to meet growing demand, and Arctic deposits represent a frontier opportunity:

  • Greenland: Skaergaard's 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces is the largest gold resource in the Arctic region outside of Russia
  • Canada: The Canadian Arctic and sub-Arctic host several significant gold mines (Meadowbank, Meliadine, Agnico Eagle operations)
  • Alaska: Fort Knox and other Alaska operations demonstrate the viability of Arctic gold mining
  • Scandinavia: Kittila (Finland) and other Nordic operations prove that cold-climate gold mining is commercially viable

Arctic gold supply faces development challenges (infrastructure, climate, logistics) but is increasingly competitive at current gold prices above US$5,100/oz (Feb 2026).

Skaergaard's Gold in This Context

Skaergaard's gold contribution takes on additional significance in the context of central bank-driven demand:

  • Physical gold: Central banks want physical gold, not paper exposure. New mine supply that delivers physical gold to the market directly addresses this demand
  • Western supply: Central bank buyers include Western nations (Poland, Singapore) that may prefer gold from Western-aligned jurisdictions
  • Scale: Skaergaard's gold endowment is large enough to make a meaningful contribution to Western gold supply if developed
  • Price tailwind: If central bank buying continues at current rates, gold prices are likely to remain elevated, improving Skaergaard's gold economics

Beyond Central Banks: Other Demand Drivers

While central bank buying is the dominant demand story, other factors support gold demand:

  • Retail investment: Gold ETFs and bar and coin demand remain significant
  • Jewelry: The largest demand category, particularly in India and China
  • Technology: Gold has unique properties (conductivity, corrosion resistance) that maintain demand from electronics and other industries
  • De-dollarization: Broader trends toward reducing dependence on US dollar-denominated assets support gold as an alternative store of value

Conclusion

Central bank gold buying is a structural demand driver that shows no signs of abating. This creates a favorable environment for gold prices and for new gold supply development. Skaergaard's 23.5 million gold-equivalent ounces positions it to contribute to Western gold supply at a time when demand from official sector buyers is absorbing an unprecedented share of global production. For gold-focused investors, Skaergaard offers exposure to Arctic gold supply that benefits directly from the central bank buying trend.
