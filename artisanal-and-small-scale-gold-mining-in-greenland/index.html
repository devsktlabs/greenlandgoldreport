Artisanal and Small-Scale Gold Mining in Greenland

By Erik Johansson · 2026-03-23

artisanal mininggreenlandasgmlocal economy

Artisanal and small-scale gold mining (ASGM) is a significant economic activity in many developing countries, providing livelihoods for millions of people. In Greenland, the ASGM sector is small but has historical roots and potential relevance to the territory's broader gold mining development.

ASGM in Greenland: Historical Context

Gold has been known to exist in Greenland for centuries, and informal gold panning and small-scale mining has occurred sporadically throughout Greenlandic history. Indigenous Greenlandic populations collected gold from alluvial deposits for decorative purposes, and early European settlers conducted small-scale gold exploration.

In more recent times, artisanal-scale gold activity has been limited. The harsh Arctic climate, small population, and the dominance of commercial exploration by international mining companies have constrained the growth of a significant ASGM sector. Greenland does not have the large ASGM populations found in countries like Ghana, Burkina Faso, or Peru.

Current ASGM Activity

Current artisanal and small-scale gold activity in Greenland is minimal:

  • **Alluvial gold**: Some alluvial gold occurrences are known in southern and western Greenland, and occasional recreational panning occurs. However, commercial-scale alluvial mining has not been established
  • **Ruby mining**: Greenland's most significant small-scale mining activity is ruby mining at the Aappaluttoq deposit, which is more closely related to gemstone mining than gold mining
  • **Regulatory framework**: Greenland's Mineral Resources Act applies to all mining activity regardless of scale, meaning that even small-scale gold miners must obtain permits

    • The regulatory requirement for permits creates a barrier for informal ASGM activity, as the permitting process may be perceived as too complex or costly for small operators.

    ASGM vs Industrial Mining: The Greenland Context

    The relationship between ASGM and industrial mining in Greenland differs from that in many developing countries:

  • **Limited ASGM presence**: Unlike many African, South American, and Asian gold-producing countries, Greenland does not have a large existing ASGM population that would be affected by the development of industrial-scale mining
  • **No ASGM conflict**: Industrial mining projects like Skaergaard are unlikely to face conflicts with ASGM operators over land access or resource ownership, as these conflicts are minimal in Greenland
  • **Complementary relationship**: If ASGM activity grows in the future, it could complement industrial mining by providing local employment and economic diversification

    • Potential for ASGM Development

    Several factors could support the growth of ASGM in Greenland:

  • **Exploration data**: Commercial exploration companies generate geological data that could be used by small-scale prospectors to identify gold targets
  • **Rising gold prices**: Higher gold prices make even small deposits economic for artisanal miners
  • **Skills development**: Industrial mining operations could train local workers in mining skills that could later be applied in ASGM activities
  • **Technology**: Improved gold recovery technology (sluice boxes, centrifugal concentrators) makes small-scale gold recovery more efficient

    • Challenges for ASGM in Greenland

    Significant challenges constrain ASGM growth:

  • **Climate**: The Arctic climate limits the field season for alluvial mining and makes year-round small-scale operations impractical
  • **Access**: Remote gold occurrences are difficult and expensive to access without industrial-scale logistics
  • **Permafrost**: Frozen ground complicates alluvial mining techniques that rely on water-based processing
  • **Market access**: Small-scale gold producers may struggle to access formal gold markets, refineries, and buyers
  • **Environmental compliance**: Environmental regulations that apply to all mining activity may be difficult for small operators to comply with

    • Lessons from Other Arctic Jurisdictions

    Canada's northern territories provide some insight into how ASGM operates in Arctic environments:

  • **Yukon placer mining**: The Yukon has a long history of placer gold mining, with both industrial and small-scale operators. The sector is regulated by the Yukon government with specific provisions for placer mining
  • **Nunavut**: ASGM activity in Nunavut is minimal, similar to Greenland, due to the remote location and limited population
  • **Alaska**: Alaska has a significant recreational and small-scale gold mining sector, supported by accessible gold-bearing streams and a regulatory framework that accommodates small operators

    • Greenland could consider adopting elements of the Yukon or Alaska models if it wishes to encourage small-scale gold activity.

    Formalization and Regulation

    If ASGM grows in Greenland, formalization will be important to ensure:

  • **Environmental protection**: Small-scale operations comply with environmental standards
  • **Tax and royalty collection**: Gold production is properly taxed and reported
  • **Safety**: Miners operate safely in hazardous Arctic conditions
  • **Market access**: Formal miners can access legitimate gold buyers and refiners

    • Conclusion

    ASGM is not currently a significant factor in Greenland's gold sector, and industrial projects like Skaergaard face no meaningful conflict with artisanal miners. The Arctic environment, small population, and regulatory requirements constrain ASGM growth. However, rising gold prices and the development of industrial mining infrastructure could create future opportunities for small-scale gold activity. Greenland's approach to ASGM regulation should balance economic opportunity with environmental and safety standards, drawing on the experience of other Arctic jurisdictions.
